Title: Fort Greene Wishing Tree
Project by: Rachel Gorman and Mariel Soto Reyes


Original presentation slide deck: HERE

Background

Intial Assignment

Our initial assignment was to design, create, and install an interactive object or experience that was compelling and clear enough that members of the public would engage with it correctly without written text or verbal instruction.

Creative Process

Location Research and Activity Mapping

After surveying popular public gathering spaces via Google Maps, we decided to focus our research and activities on Fort Greene Park, in Brooklyn, NY.

Fort Greene Park has been a Brooklyn landmark for more than 150 years. Originally the site was cleared for a military fort that was used by American soldiers in the Revolutionary War. After the War of 1812 was over, citizens began to use the space as an informal gathering place until the City of Brooklyn designated the area as a public park in 1845. Walt Whitman is on record supporting the park, saying that it would become “a place of recreation...where, on hot summer evenings, and Sundays, they can spend a few grateful hours in the enjoyment of wholesome rest and fresh air."

Today, the 30-acre park is neatly landscaped with playgrounds, tennis courts, and paved paths for people to enjoy. Because the park is so large, we began by creating heat maps of the space. We wanted to identify the areas of the park that people tended to gather, because we believe that locating our project in a popular area will improve its chances of interaction.

After Mariel heat mapped the park, we determined that a large expanse at the corner of Dekalb St. and Washington Park Ave. would be the ideal location to center our activities.

Once we had decided on an area of focus, I created behavioral maps and AEIOU studies on Sunday evening and Monday morning to get a sense of the kinds of people that visited the area of the park, what they did, and how they interacted with the space.

My behavioral maps used symbols to convey the nature of the action and lines to delineate how people move through space.

AIEOUs were limited to three groups of people most active in the space while it was surveyed.

After mapping the area at different times a day, we discussed our takeaways and discovered, among other things, that:

  1. 1. Adults tended to use the environment as it was prescribed (walking on the path, sitting on benches) while children were more likely to interact with the natural parts of the environment/explore.
  2. 2. Most adults were either accompanied by a handful of peers or by their children. Most were insular and focused on interacting with themselves, though some would interact with the environment if they were following their kids’ lead.
  3. 3. Children tended to gravitate towards a large sandpit in the center of the observed area of the park and to a large rock on the edge of the observed area of the park, running between them (and dragging their parents with them).

Brainstorm & Concept

Once we had a sense of the park’s visitors and their interactions with the environment, we talked about the essential nature of the park, and the line it treads between the artificial and natural.

We also touched on how we wanted to make people feel. Namely, that we wanted to create a sense of wonder and whimsy, to inspire feelings of natural abundance, and to play with the audience's expectations of the environment — to create something that both “gelled” with the surrounding area and was a departure from it.

Finally, we teased out what action we wanted to encourage people to take. Harvesting, or pulling things off of an artificial tree, seemed like a direction that fit with our overall vision.

As we brainstormed, we thought through the kinds of edible and inedible objects that would compel visitors to harvest. As a part of our creative process, we got feedback from our classmates. Harvestable objects under consideration included:

  • 1. Edible Fruit
  • 2. Unwrapped candy
  • 3. Wrapped candy
  • 4. Paper fortunes

Ultimately we decided to create a whimsical tree festooned with fortunes that were made out of seed paper to cut down on waste and add an extra element to our design. We decided to paint the tree pink to call attention to its artificiality and attract maxium attention.

We also decided that we would place the tree in a popular area of the park, between two trees, near a highly-trafficked path for maximum visibility.
