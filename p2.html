Final Project

Sketching and Observational Exercise

Considerations and Initial Questions

I am interested in exploring about how design and technology can use synchronization to help people regulate their physiology and feel more comfortable and at ease. Initial guiding questions include:

  1. 1. How can I help people feel calmer? More centered and aligned, less anxious and tense?
  2. 2. How can I create a technology that feels comfortable or puts people at ease? Can I create a technology that feels inimate and gentle, empathetic and kind?
  3. 3. How can technology help people soothe and self-regulate, meditate or be more mindful?
  4. 4. Is there a way to synch ourselves to a healthier rhythm or better breathing pattern?
  5. 5. If we feel something else's breathing, does our own breathing change?
  6. 6. Can you power something with your body's electricity?
  7. 7. Can we create tech that mimics the feeling of being around friendly animals or pets?
  8. 8. Can we use biomaterials to achieve my objective?

Research

To begin, I wanted to examine whether people were as anxious or tense as I presumed and what the nature of their anxieties were. I also observed my partner and my own behavior over a period of time to see if we engaged in self-regulating activities (and what those activities were).

In the end, the experiment was not successful. No matter how much air I blew into the inflatable, it was too large and never filled with air.

Takeaways

I was really disappointed with this one. Before I began, I thought that I had found a way forward with this project, and that this final piece would be an effective amalgamation of all that I have learned over the course of the week. Instead, it was a miserable failure. I'm glad this isn't 8 in 8 because I am really not sure where or how to proceed from here.

I was able to ask my husband for feedback about whether the cotton fleece or rayon velvet felt better to him. He liked the velvet because it was multi-directional and he felt that encouranged him to "pet" the pillow as a way to self-soothe.

Prototype 1

After a long and arduous 7-in-7, I was still searching for a form and function that would align with my investigatory interests and creative values. I took note of my classmates' feedback and began to consider the implications of creating something that was comfortable, vs. something that held people to account.

After that brainstorm, I surveyed friends and family about what they might prefer to experience, and choose to design a product that would comfort individuals, rather than challenge them. I thought, given the state of the world, I should focus on making people more comfortable, rather than less. With that creative goal in mind, I began to experiment again, essentially creating a series of second "7-in-7" style prototypes to see if I couldn't find a unique direction to pursue.

Feedback and Next Steps

Unfortunately for my pillow idea, it wasn't received very well. The class rightly pointed out that it looked out of place in a home and that it was too similar to other products already on the market. They challenged me to think more experimentally, to consider how I could push the non-human, mammalian physiology aspect more significantly, and think about how much more I could do. I went back to the drawing board and began to brainstorm again.

Prototype 1.5

Tasked with reconsidering my concept, I revisited the idea of making my project more critical, and came up with some ideas that I thought could visually communicate my concerns with modern human life, and particularly, our troublesome relationship with animals. I wanted to continue to consider comfort, but to think about it through a fine art lens, creating either an experimental form to showcase a new kind of comfort, or by using this project to explore the uncomfortable environmental truths that help us maintain our phsysical ease and sense of well-being.

Form Exploration

New ideas for forms that I could build for my final project included:

  • 1. A chaise lounge that operated to reveal animal skeletons at it's base, speaking to the animal suffering that supports our current, comfortable modern lifestyles.
  • 2. a large, plushy chair shaped like a pile of meerkats.
  • 3.A hammock made of plastic bags on the outside and plushy material on the inside, speaking to our need to find an organic way to live within a manufactured world.
  • 4. A shelter where expectations were flipped: the exterior was polished and modern, and the interior wild and animalistic. I thought it could speak to the costs and benefits of living seperately from the rest of the animal kingdom.

    After speaking with Harpreet about my potential directions, I settled on creating a shelter that took a critical view of our modern life by investigating the intersection between modern comfort and existential angst.

    Material exploration

    Because I wanted the exterior to be modern and industrial, I decided to create a cubic form. Because I wanted the interior to evoke a feeling of both coziness and dread, I decided to take inspiration from mammalian predator dens, specifically those of hibernating bears. Like a bear den, I wanted people to experience entering the structure and feeling struck by the interior being immediately cozy and supremely comfortable. That feeling would then be slowly supplanted by a growing anxiety and gnawing dread. For that reason, I decided to use faux fur on the interior and to create two massive furry shapes that people could lay on or in-between. I also decided to use audio to enhance the feeling of the space and, referencing my past research on co-regulation, take the participant on the emotional journey.

    I wanted the exterior to contrast heavily with the interior, and came up with a number of materials that I thought might be meaningful and visually interesting. They included:

    • 1. Bubblewrap: Padding that could speak to our plastic present.
    • 2. Acrylic sheets: Slick, minimalist, modern, and beautiful, but quite plasticine and wholly removed from nature.
    • 3. Mirror: Reflecting not only the comfortable-but-anxious modern world quite literally, but also speaking to the idea that we take comfort in our human body being "different" than other animals or natural systems.
    • 4. Cardboard: specifically, an Amazon box. Reflecting the comfort we take in modern convenience, even when it's unsustainable.
    • 5. White plastic Bags: Similar to cardboard, convenience-as-comfort, even though it is awful for us.
    • 6. Gummy Bears: Colorful, glossy, sweet figures we take pleasure in but also symbolize our disastrous, often lethal relationship with sugar and manufactured foods. I liked thinking about how we've reduced natural predators to cartoon characters that kill us as we eat them for comfort. I also thought that the material/bear form contrast would be interesting.

      Prototype 2

      Survey results indicated that most people could look at a mirrored surface and self-generate a meaning that was similar to my artistic intent. I decided to move forward with a reflective, silver surface for that reason, and because I was excited by the idea that it could be read both symbolically and literally as a "reflection" of our modern lifestyles.

      For my prototype 2, I created two more forms. One with a solid reflective surface and one with cut outs that were meant to echo the shape of windows, as if it was a city building. I brought my two prototypes into my class, and had my classmates help me decide which was the more appealing and more meaningful direction for me to take.

      Final Build-Out

      Once I had decided on a form, exterior material, interior material and set up, and audio experience, it was time to build the structure. I wanted the space to feel comfortable and as accessible as possible, so I decided to make a shelter that was 5'x5'. In the interest of time, in an effort to keep the structure easy to relocate, and in order to be cost-effective, I built the frame out of 2 in. x 3 in. lumber, and the walls out of a twinwall polycarbonate material that I hand-applied a chromatic vinyl to in order to make the exterior surface reflective. The interior was created with 3" foam and pillow fill.

      After noting participant survey answers (and speaking to people 1:1), it is clear that the structure succeeded in that people attached the appropriate meanings and associations to its exterior, and went through the emotional journey that I had hoped they would. There are still some elements that need further iteration, though.

      If I were to move forward with this piece, I would:

      • 1. Re-consider the interior (a portion of the project that was hard to test before full build-out)to make sure that people more closely associated it with organic, wild, natural environments and animals.
      • 2. Allow people to customize the intensity of their experience: some people reported after their experience that the sound was unbearable. Others suggested that I should ramp it up further.
      • 3. Add a tunnel or curtain to allow maximum sensory deprivation and a full-body immersive experience.

      Addendum

      After I presented my final project in class, the visiting professors gave me food for thought about the future direction of this piece. They suggested that I consider:

      • 1. Looking into human-made artificial habitats for animals that wouldn't survive without human intervention (or are otherwise wholly dependent on humans for their survival now), and consider how they can serve as a counterpoint to, or enhancement of, the project as it currently exists.
      • 2. Recasting this project as a speculative piece, rather than a momentary fine art experience. Thinking about "outside rooms" as a concept that people could build into their homes in a future with less access to natural spaces. Keeping my materials DIY and presenting my speculative concept as a fine-art piece as to avoid negative product-oriented associations.
